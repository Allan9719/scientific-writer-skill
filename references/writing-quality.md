## Foundational Principles

1. "The aim is to make it **impossible for the reader not to understand**."
2. "Data and information alone have no intrinsic function without a narrative."
3. "Good organisation and good writing can compensate for language errors, but error-free language does not compensate for poor organisation or poor writing."
4. "Everything in the text should be consistent with and lead towards the destination point — the 'magnetic south'."
5. "Knowing WHAT to write is not the same as knowing HOW to write it."

## Paragraphing

- **Average paragraph length** in research journals: 150–170 words
- A paragraph with a **single function** is more successful than one with multiple functions
- Start with a narrative entry statement:
  - "We now consider the connection between..."
  - "To address this question, we used..."
  - "Taken together, these studies suggest that..."
  - "There are two potential alternatives to such an approach..."
- Avoid statements early in the paragraph that won't make sense until the reader sees the rest
- Don't add "loose sentences" irrelevant to the paragraph function

## Sentence-Level Issues

### Buried Predicates

Count words between subject and main verb. If more than ~10–12 words, the predicate is buried.

- Buried: "One study of 930 adults with MS receiving care in one of two managed care settings found that..."
- Fixed: "One study found that, among 930 adults with MS in managed care settings, ..."

### Sentence Length

- Average in journals: **20–26 words**
- Over-long sentences become "flat" — the important part is submerged
- Consecutive long sentences are particularly problematic
- Risk factors: more than one "and," more than one "which," too many prepositions, too many nouns

### Sentence Start-up

- Connect sentences with overlapping repeats, pro-forms (This/These + noun), or signalling connectors (However)
- Identify important information by beginning with: "Interestingly," "Remarkably," "It is noteworthy that"
- Avoid beginning with **-ing forms** or **prepositions** (especially "for" and "with")

### "and" / "or" Ambiguity

These connectors can drag things you don't want, or fail to drag things you do want:

> "We found an increase in demand and deployment of non-renewable energy sources."
> Did demand AND deployment both increase? Or two things were found: (1) an increase in demand and (2) deployment — which did not increase?

### Signalling Connectors

Connectors are **not just glue** — they are emphatic with specific meanings. Wrong connector = reader goes in wrong direction.

- **"therefore"** — is it really a direct consequence?
- **"for example"** — does it genuinely illustrate the previous statement?
- **"in other words"** — is it really the same thing restated?

## Grammar Pitfalls

### -ing Ambiguity

-ing forms are inherently ambiguous — they have **no tense** and **no number marker**, and can have **10 possible meanings**:

| Meaning | Replacement |
|---------|-------------|
| by [verb]ing | "by forming" |
| as a result of [verb]ing | "as a result of forming" |
| on the basis of [verb]ing | "on the basis of forming" |
| when [verb]ing | "when forming" |
| thereby [verb]ing | "thereby forming" |
| therefore [verb]ing | "therefore forming" |
| which is/are capable of [verb]ing | "which can form" |
| which/that [verb] | "which form" |
| if we/it/they [verb] | "if they form" |
| because it/they [verb] | "because they form" |

> Error: "MTCs are deposits resulting from creep, slide, slump and flow processes **producing** a variety of rheological units and strain sequences **encapsulating** extensional, translational and compressional domains."

Who is producing? What is encapsulating? By/when/thereby/which? All unclear.

> Error: "Membranes remain flat **storing** elastic curvature stress."
> By storing? When storing? Thereby storing?

**Fix**: Replace -ing forms with explicit connectors ("by storing," "thereby storing," "when storing").

### Adverb Placement

Adverbs like "just," "only," "simply" **change meaning depending on position**:

| Position | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| **Only** we analysed models... | No other researchers did this |
| We **only** analysed models... | We didn't do anything else |
| We analysed **only** models... | We didn't analyse anything else |
| ...can **only** simulate... | Models can do nothing else |
| ...can simulate **only** the... | Models simulate just one thing |
| ...the patient's **only** response | Patient had just one response |

### Ownership Ambiguity

Sentences with non-human subjects + Present Simple risk being read as common knowledge rather than your contribution:

> Ambiguous: "Kinetic analyses suggest that these probes are active site directed..."
> (Is this established knowledge or your finding? The reader can't tell.)

**Fix with one of these strategies:**
1. Use Past Simple or Present Perfect: "We **have synthesized**..."
2. Use active rather than passive: "**We** designed..."
3. Use a human grammatical subject: "**Our results** demonstrate..."
4. Add explicit reference: "**This study** shows..." / "**Here**, we show..."

### Preposition Problems

Prepositions change meaning: **"evidence of"** vs. **"evidence for"**; **"improved up to 3 times"** vs. **"improved by up to 3 times"**

**"with" is particularly ambiguous** — consider replacing with: "using," "having," "in combination with," "together with," "as a result of," "at the same time as."

Strings of prepositional phrases create serious ambiguity:
> "We apply **in** A the concept **of** B **to** C **through** D **with** E to constrain the timing **of** F **for** G."

**"for the first time"** placement determines what is novel:
> "X-ray imaging was used to measure contact angles within oil-bearing rocks at reservoir conditions **for the first time**."
> First time for what? X-ray imaging? Measuring contact angles? Within oil-bearing rocks? At reservoir conditions?

### Reference Clarity

- Add a noun to **this/these/that/those**: "this system," not just "this"
- Replace **it/which/this** with the actual noun when multiple referents exist
- Check where the referent begins and ends — is it clear to the reader?

### Subject-Verb Agreement

- Check if the subject can **logically perform** the verb's action
- Error: "The nature of the problem decided how to resolve it." — a "nature" cannot "decide"

### Article Usage

Both "a" and "the" can be grammatically correct but change meaning:
- "The cords should be connected to **a** two outlets" (unclear) vs. "**the** two outlets" (specific)

## Vocabulary Accuracy

### Consistency

Use terminology consistently. A "tool" should not become a "strategy," then a "device," then an "approach," then a "methodology," then a "framework," then a "technique."

### The Thesaurus Trap

No two words have exactly the same meaning in every context. "Noticeable" is not "conspicuous." "Famous" is not "well-known." **Don't be afraid to repeat the same word** if it makes reading easier.

### Weak Verbs to Avoid

| Weak | Specific Alternatives |
|------|----------------------|
| have | possess, contain, include |
| get | obtain, achieve, become |
| bring | provide, yield, cause |
| keep | retain, maintain, conserve |
| spread | distribute, diffuse, scatter, extend |

### Commonly Confused Pairs

| Pair | Distinction |
|------|-------------|
| alternately / alternatively | "one after the other" vs. "on the other hand" |
| beside / besides | "next to" vs. "apart from / in addition to" |
| criterion / criteria | **singular** / **plural** |
| different / various | "not the same" vs. "a range of" |
| e.g. / i.e. | "for example" vs. "in other words" |
| effective / efficient | "it works" vs. "it works well" |
| phenomenon / phenomena | **singular** / **plural** |
| adapt / adopt | "modify/adjust" vs. "choose to use" |
| affect / effect | "influence" vs. "cause/bring about" |
| imply / infer | "suggest/indicate" vs. "conclude/deduce" |

### Hyphenation Changes Meaning

- "five centimetre-wide layers" = five layers, each 1 cm wide
- "five-centimetre wide layers" = each layer is 5 cm wide

## Self-Editing Tics

### Lexical Tics

Overused words that dilute meaning: **indeed**, **in fact**, **basically**, **clearly**. Flag and remove unless they serve a specific purpose.

### Punctuation Tics

**Dash pairs** — meaning is not obvious, especially to international readers. Consider translating what you mean by dash pairs into explicit language.

**Parentheses** — if something is important enough to include, perhaps it shouldn't be in parentheses. If it's not important enough, perhaps it shouldn't be there at all.

**Commas** — using commas to stuff more information into a sentence often creates over-long, rambling sentences. Consider breaking into separate, well-linked sentences.
